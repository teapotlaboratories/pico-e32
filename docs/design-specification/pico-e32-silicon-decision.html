REV B Silicon decision/ Target: locked 60 fps, full cart compatibility/ WiFi retained

Faster core, not more cores

You asked whether to add a co-processor. The arithmetic answers before the engineering does: the thing you would need to send across the wire does not fit in a frame, and the things that do fit are already cheap on a core you are not using.

Rev A asked whether the ESP32-S3 could run PICO-8 at all, and said yes — at 30 fps, with 60 as a per-cart bonus. ▸ You have since raised the bar to locked 60 fps on everything real PICO-8 runs. That is a different question, and it has a different answer.
NO

Do not add a compute co-processor.

Not an RP2040, not an RP2350, not a second ESP32. It is ruled out a priori by the arithmetic above — there is nothing to give it. Don't prototype it.

YES

Move to the ESP32-P4, with a C6 for radio.

~2.0× the single-thread throughput and ~2.2× the PSRAM bandwidth — the only two axes that matter. The P4 has no radio, so choosing it is choosing a co-processor architecture. Just the right kind.

BUT FIRST

A Pi Zero 2 W meets your bar today.

$15, WiFi on board, runs Lexaloffle's official binary at 60 fps with zero compatibility caveats. If "every cart at 60" is a requirement rather than a craft goal, the MCU path is the harder road by choice.

01

You already have a spare core, and it's bored

The intuition behind "add a co-processor" is that the S3 is out of compute. It isn't. It is out of single-thread compute, on one core, in a loop that cannot be split. Meanwhile the other core spends most of the frame doing nothing.

Why the "display list" split doesn't rescue it either

The cleverest version of the co-processor idea is a command FIFO: let Lua run on the S3, stream spr/map/line/rectfill calls over SPI, and let a second chip rasterise. The bandwidth works — a heavy cart issues low-tens-of-KB of draw commands per frame, which crosses in under a millisecond.

It still doesn't help. The draw calls are issued by the Lua code, interleaved with the state reads that make them slow. You would offload the rasteriser — which is C++, already fast, and already on the idle core — while leaving the interpreter exactly where it was. You'd have built a SuperFX for a console whose bottleneck isn't pixels.

THE HISTORICAL ANALOGY BREAKS

The SNES SuperFX, the Sega 32X, and the Amiga blitter all worked because the host was the fast part and the co-processor took bounded, embarrassingly-parallel pixel work. PICO-8 is the inverse: the slow part is the interpreter, and the interpreter is precisely the part you cannot move.

02

What a faster core actually buys

Two numbers govern a Lua interpreter chasing pointers through a 2 MB heap: how fast one core retires branchy integer code, and how fast that core can reach memory. Everything else is noise.

03

The two axes, and the zero-length vector

Plot every option on the only two axes that govern this workload. The co-processor idea has a specific, visible failure: it moves you nowhere.

04

Do not architect around the PPA

The P4's Pixel-Processing Accelerator sounds like it was built for this. It wasn't. It cannot do PICO-8's single most characteristic pixel operation, and the reason is a detail buried in the ESP-IDF reference.

05

The co-processor that is justified

Here is the twist. The P4 has no radio. You said WiFi is preferable. So choosing the P4 means adding a second chip anyway — and it collapses your two questions into one.

07

The argument you should hear before you spend a month

You raised the bar to "everything real PICO-8 runs, at 60 fps." There is a $15 board that clears that bar today, by running the actual program. It deserves a fair hearing before you write a line of C++.

Raspberry Pi Zero 2 W

Quad Cortex-A53 @ 1 GHz · 512 MB LPDDR2 · WiFi + BT on board · ~$15 · Lexaloffle's own supported target

WHAT YOU GET

  • The official PICO-8 binary. Not a reimplementation. Zero compatibility caveats, zero cart-by-cart triage, zero "fake-08 doesn't implement fill patterns yet."
  • Locked 60 fps on everything. The bar you set is met on day one, not after a benchmark and a gate.
  • WiFi and Splore. Browse and download BBS carts on the device, the way PICO-8 intends.
  • Weeks of your life back. No host layer, no z8lua cross-compile, no SDIO coexistence debugging.

WHAT YOU GIVE UP

  • Instant-on. A console turns on. A Linux box boots — multi-second, and you will feel it every single time you pick the thing up.
  • Battery life. An idling OS draws meaningfully more than a sleeping MCU. Your runtime numbers get worse.
  • The build. If part of the point is that you made a console — bare metal, one binary, no operating system — the Pi takes that away.
  • Determinism. An MCU frame loop is yours. A Linux scheduler's isn't.
The honest framing: the MCU path is not the practical answer to "run every cart at 60 fps." It is the answer to "build a console." Those are both legitimate goals, but they are different goals, and only you know which one you actually have. If it's the first, buy the Pi. If it's the second — and the Rev A report suggests it is — then read on, and go P4.
08

Can the S3 still be saved?

Partly. There is real headroom left in software — enough to matter, probably not enough to lock 60 fps on the hardest carts. Here is every lever, honestly rated.

THE REALISTIC S3 OUTCOME

Stack the shippable levers and you plausibly get most 30 fps carts running beautifully and many 60 fps carts running well — with a tail of heavy _update60 titles dropping to 30. PICO-8 itself degrades exactly this way when stat(1) exceeds 1.0, so it is not a broken device.

It just isn't the bar you set. If you hold to "everything, at 60," the S3 does not get there on software alone, and no amount of extra silicon bolted alongside it changes that.

09

The decision

One benchmark decides this, and you can run it on a $55 board. Everything before it is argument; everything after it is engineering.

WHAT WOULD CHANGE THIS ANSWER

■ A maintained RV32 bare-metal LuaJIT backend for the P4. Today the RISC-V port targets RV64 Linux, so you are running a plain interpreter and CoreMark is the governing metric. A JIT would blow the whole question open.

■ S3 Gate-2 coming back at ≤13 ms on your heaviest carts, with bytecode + SRAM arena + 120 MHz PSRAM. Then the single-chip S3 survives and you skip the C6 and the SDIO complexity entirely — at the cost of relying on an experimental PSRAM mode.

■ Your actual cart list turning out to contain few heavy _update60 titles. The 60 fps carts are a minority of the library. If the ones you care about aren't in it, the entire headroom argument evaporates and the S3 is fine.

10

What is measured, what is derived, what is a guess

MEASURED · TRUST THESE

  • CoreMark from vendor datasheets: S3 single-core 613.86 @ 240 MHz; P4 dual-core 2489.62 @ 360 MHz.
  • PSRAM bandwidth: S3 ~84 MB/s exceeding cache; P4 ~185 MB/s @ 200 MHz; RP2350 ~16–46 MB/s QSPI.
  • WiFi over SDIO: ~36 Mbps real throughput, ~79.5 Mbps link, ~3% esp_hosted overhead.
  • PicoPico's Celeste frame times, and the fixed-point synth win (~25 ms → ~2 ms per SFX).

DERIVED · THE ARITHMETIC IS SOUND, THE INPUTS ARE ASSUMPTIONS

  • The "~2× Lua speedup" for the P4 is inferred from CoreMark plus memory characteristics. Nobody has published a Lua benchmark on both chips. Gate 2 exists precisely to replace this estimate with a number.
  • P4 single-core CoreMark (~1245) is the dual-core figure halved. The S3's dual-core is only 1.93× its single-core, so the true P4 single-core number is probably a little higher than 1245, not lower.
  • The ~20 µs cross-chip round-trip in the hero figure is an engineering estimate. The conclusion survives any plausible value — at 5 µs it's still 3.5 ms for 1% of instructions.
  • The frame-anatomy figure is illustrative. No per-stage breakdown of a heavy _update60 cart on an S3 has ever been published. The topology is certain; the proportions are not.

UNRESOLVED · GO IN WITH EYES OPEN

  • Datasheet conflict on the S3. Dual-core CoreMark is 1181.60 in datasheet v1.x and 1329.92 in v2.2 — a 13% revision, probably a toolchain change. It doesn't move the verdict, but it should make you suspicious of any single number.
  • P4 clock. Most shipping boards run 360 MHz, not the 400 MHz spec ceiling. Benchmark at whatever your board actually clocks, not what the datasheet promises.
  • No shipping PICO-8 port on the P4. RetroGo and DOOM prove the chip and the toolchain, but you will be the first to take fake-08 there — and the first to hit whatever is waiting.
  • The PPA/A4-mode finding is a reading of the current ESP-IDF reference. Verify against your IDF version before you architect around it — in either direction.
