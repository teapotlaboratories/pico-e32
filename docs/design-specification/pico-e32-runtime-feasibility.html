Feasibility Report/ Fantasy console on a microcontroller/ Rev A

Running PICO‑8 carts on an ESP32‑S3

The question isn't whether the chip can boot a Lua VM. It's whether 240 MHz of Xtensa and 84 MB/s of PSRAM can hold a 16.6 millisecond frame. Here is what the measurement says.

SYSTEM PALETTE · 16
VERDICT

Feasible. Ship 30 fps, treat 60 as a bonus.

Real carts run — Celeste at 9 ms/frame on a slower LX6 core proves it. But the PSRAM wall and the 2 MB Lua heap mean heavy _update60 carts will not lock. Design the device around 30 fps and let the good carts overshoot.

HARDWARE

You need 8 MB of Octal PSRAM.

PICO-8's Lua heap is capped at 2 MB. The board on your bench has exactly 2 MB of PSRAM total — zero margin. Target an ESP32-S3-WROOM-1-N16R8. Your 4-inch board already has one, which is the right call.

DE-RISK FIRST

Two things can kill this. Test both this week.

Lua-on-Xtensa throughput, and PSRAM framebuffer bandwidth. Everything else — audio, input, power, enclosure — is solved engineering. Do not design a PCB until these two return green.

01

Somebody already did the hard part

Nobody has shipped a polished ESP32-S3 PICO-8 handheld. But two projects between them have already answered the two questions that matter, and a third gives you a runtime you can port instead of write.

PicoPico (DavidVentura) is the proof. It runs Sam Hocevar's z8lua — PICO-8's Lua dialect with a native 16.16 fixed-point number type — on an ESP32 with 4 MB of PSRAM, and reports Celeste at roughly 9 ms per frame with rendering on the second core and SFX included. It only works because of three specific decisions you should copy: precompile Lua to bytecode (cart parse fell from 112 ms to 18 ms), convert the audio synth to fixed point (one SFX went from ~25 ms to ~2 ms), and put the Lua heap in PSRAM — which is exactly why the RP2040 port was abandoned.

fake-08 (jtothebell, MIT) is the runtime you want. It is a clean-room C++ reimplementation of the PICO-8 VM with genuinely broad cart compatibility, and it already runs on 3DS, Switch, Vita, Wii U, Miyoo Mini and even the Nintendo DS. Critically, it isolates every platform assumption behind a host layer (SDL2Host.cpp, 3dsHost.cpp…). Your job is to write one more: ESP32Host. It already runs on a 268 MHz ARM11 — a fair proxy for a 240 MHz Xtensa.

DECISION

Port fake-08. Do not write a runtime. Reimplementing the PICO-8 API surface, the .p8.png steganography, and the PXA cart decompression is years of work that fake-08 already did. Write the ESP32Host layer — framebuffer flush, audio callback, input bitmask, SD file access, timing — and steal PicoPico's three optimisations wholesale.

02

Two memory maps, one of which does not fit

PICO-8's addressable memory is a rounding error — 32 KB, smaller than the S3's cache. The problem is the memory PICO-8 doesn't put on the map: a Lua heap capped at 2 MiB, entirely separate from the 32 KB, and checkable at runtime with stat(0).

PICO-8's own performance model, and what it costs you

PICO-8 defines a virtual CPU of 8,388,608 cycles per second (223) — about 139,810 cycles per frame at 60 fps, or 279,620 at 30. At roughly two cycles per Lua VM instruction, that is a target of around 4.2 million Lua VM instructions per second. That is a deliberately modest bar; Lexaloffle's own stated minimum spec is a ~700 MHz Raspberry Pi.

The two things that would normally make this impossible on an MCU are both already solved:

  • Float math. z8lua redefines LUA_NUMBER as a 16.16 fixed-point type (z8::fix32). Every arithmetic op in a cart therefore bypasses the FPU entirely. PicoPico's microbenchmark: 32,000 sin calls dropped from ~735 ms to ~135 ms once fixed-point.
  • Cart parse time. Precompile Lua source to bytecode at load. PicoPico: 112 ms → 18 ms.
  • LuaJIT is not available. There is no Xtensa backend. You are running the standard Lua 5.x interpreter, and no amount of wishing changes that.
03

The PSRAM wall

Octal PSRAM at 80 MHz DDR should deliver 160 MB/s. Measured, once your working set exceeds the cache, it delivers around 84. That single number decides your display architecture.

CONSEQUENCE

The 480×480 panel is the better screen and the worse bus. If Phase 2 shows the RGB stream starving the Lua heap, your options are: drop to 30 fps panel refresh (halves the stream to ~13.8 MB/s), enable the 120 MHz PSRAM option, or move to an i80/QSPI panel. Decide with a measurement, not a preference.

04

128 pixels do not divide into everything

A fantasy console with blurred pixels is a broken fantasy console. Integer scaling is not a preference here, it is a requirement — and it disqualifies more panels than you would expect.

How to actually do the scale

Never allocate a full-size RGB565 framebuffer if you can avoid it. The efficient pattern, and the one PicoPico uses:

  • Keep the native 128×128 4-bit indexed framebuffer in SRAM (8 KB). This is what the PICO-8 API draws into.
  • Pre-encode the 16 palette entries as uint16_t RGB565 once, at boot.
  • Per output line: look up, expand, and horizontally replicate into a small RGB565 line buffer in SRAM. Repeat each source line N times vertically for an N× scale — no extra work, just re-send the buffer.
  • DMA the line buffer out over esp_lcd. PSRAM is never involved.

On an RGB panel this is not possible — the peripheral demands a real, resident, full-size framebuffer — which is precisely the bandwidth story in section 03.

05

Buttons, sound, and the thing that actually drains the battery

A 16-bit parallel TFT eats your GPIO alive, and Octal PSRAM permanently locks five more. You need seven buttons and you have roughly zero pins. Meanwhile the CPU you have been agonising over draws less current than the backlight.

06

Two cores, one frame

Core 0 runs the game. Core 1 gets it onto the screen and out of the speaker. The handoff between them is an 8 KB indexed buffer and a ring of audio samples — and nothing else crosses.

TOOLCHAIN

Use ESP-IDF v5.1 or newer, not Arduino-ESP32. You need menuconfig control over PSRAM mode and clock, cache line size, compiler optimisation and FreeRTOS tick rate — and Arduino hides all of it. The RGB panel “screen drift” fix in particular requires IDF ≥ 5.1, which Arduino-ESP32 v2.x cannot give you. PlatformIO with the ESP-IDF framework is fine if you want nicer dependency management; the core is still IDF.

07

The plan, and the gates that stop it

Four phases, four gates. The gates are the point: each one is a measurement that either lets you spend more time or tells you to change the design. Do not skip forward to the PCB.

Start here. Tonight.

No purchases. ILI9488 board + ESP-EYE. Four tasks, ~1–2 weeks of evenings.

  1. Get anything on the screen. Flash an ESP-IDF esp_lcd i80 example and put up colour bars on the ILI9488. The ESP-EYE is your fallback sanity check if the panel stays dark.
  2. Blit a scaled 128×128 frame and time it. Static indexed image → palette-expand → 2× integer scale → line buffer → DMA. Log the actual frame time. GATE 1 → ≥ 30 fps full-frame, or the display architecture changes.
  3. Cross-compile z8lua with fix32 for Xtensa. Build it as an IDF component. Run a tight fixed-point loop and a sin sweep. Compare against PICO-8's ~4.2 M VM inst/sec. GATE 2 → within 2–3× of target, or plan for 30 fps only / ESP32-P4.
  4. Run one trivial cart end to end. A hand-written .p8: a sprite that moves when you press a button. Nothing else. This is the moment the project becomes real.

Enclosure

3D print it. FDM or resin, one-offs and low volume — CNC only buys you metal, which you do not need. For a 4-inch panel, a landscape PSP-style layout (screen centred, controls flanking) is the ergonomic answer; a Game Boy vertical stack only works below ~3 inches. Plenty of Raspberry Pi Zero handheld shells on Printables can be re-scaled rather than drawn from nothing.

09

Licensing, briefly

The runtime is fine. The assets are not. fake-08 and zepto8 are clean-room reimplementations — fake-08 is MIT-licensed and explicitly unaffiliated with Lexaloffle — and reimplementing the PICO-8 API is generally accepted. What you must not ship is Lexaloffle's actual binary, its BIOS, or its bundled font data.

Lexaloffle's posture has been tolerant: Joseph White has not pursued the homebrew emulators, and the community norm — echoed by fake-08, muOS and ROCKNIX — is to encourage buying the real PICO-8 at $14.99. Anbernic and Miyoo devices already ship with fake-08 preinstalled precisely because it needs no purchased binary.

The sharper issue is carts, not the runtime. Many BBS carts are unlicensed or CC-BY-NC-SA. Ship the device empty. A personal or open-source build is clearly safe; a commercial one means no Lexaloffle assets, no preloaded carts, and ideally a conversation with Lexaloffle first.

NOT LEGAL ADVICE

Tolerance is not a licence, and it can be withdrawn — especially against a product with a price tag.

10

What I am not certain about

  • The 9 ms figure is from an ESP32 (LX6), not an S3 (LX7). Same clock, and the S3 should match or beat it — but nobody has published the S3 number. Treat it as a strong indicator, not a guarantee. You will be the one who measures it.
  • The ~84 MB/s PSRAM figure is one forum measurement taken while exceeding the cache. With the 120 MHz PSRAM option, careful cache-line tuning, and hot data kept in SRAM, your number will differ. Measure it on your board rather than trusting mine.
  • I have not confirmed whether fake-08 statically reserves the full 2 MB heap. The fact it runs on a 4 MB-RAM Nintendo DS strongly implies dynamic growth, but verify when you profile.
  • The parallel-TFT pin count is indicative. Exact GPIO consumption depends on the specific board's pinout and whether it exposes RD. The conclusion — that buttons cannot use direct GPIO — holds regardless.
  • fake-08's own limitations become yours. Imperfect noise-channel audio, some v0.2.2+ features (custom fonts, fill patterns) unimplemented, and flip()-based tweetcarts can be unstable. Porting inherits the bugs along with the compatibility.
  • Nobody has finished this. PicoPico has partial API coverage and no music. PeX Labs published no framerates. You are doing genuinely novel integration work, and the estimates above are estimates.
