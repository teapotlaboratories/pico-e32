# PICO-8 Handheld at Locked 60fps: ESP32-P4 vs. ESP32-S3-Plus-Co-Processor — A Technical Verdict

## TL;DR
- **Move to the ESP32-P4 (paired with an ESP32-C6 for radio).** For a target of "everything real PICO-8 runs at 60fps," the single-threaded Lua bottleneck demands a *faster core*, not more cores. The P4 delivers roughly **2× the single-thread throughput** and **~2.2× the PSRAM bandwidth** of the S3 — the only axes that matter here — while the ESP32-C6 companion preserves WiFi/BLE over SDIO with a mature, Espressif-supported stack (esp_hosted / esp_wifi_remote).
- **Your prior is correct: a compute co-processor is the wrong answer, and the arithmetic proves it.** The Lua VM is a serially-dependent, pointer-chasing walk over a 2 MiB heap — the least parallelizable workload possible. You cannot ship heap state to an RP2350/second-ESP32 and back inside a 16.6 ms frame, and the work a co-processor *could* take (palette expansion, scaling, audio, DMA) is already cheap on the S3's idle Core 1. The **one legitimate "co-processor" is a radio companion** (the C6) — which the P4 architecture forces you to add anyway, neatly collapsing the two questions into one.
- **The honest fallback is a Raspberry Pi Zero 2 W (the $15 board built on the RP3A0 SiP, quad-core Cortex-A53 @ 1 GHz, 512 MB RAM).** It runs the *official* PICO-8 binary at full 60fps with zero compatibility caveats and has WiFi built in. If "full-cart 60fps compatibility" is a hard requirement rather than a craft goal, the SBC is the low-risk path; the MCU route (P4) is the right choice only if instant-on, battery life, and the satisfaction of an MCU-class build are themselves goals.

## Key Findings

**1. The bottleneck is single-thread Lua interpretation, and it is intrinsically serial.** David Ventura's PicoPico profiling shows the character of the workload precisely: converting a hot loop's globals to locals cut a frame from 71 ms → 29 ms, and the pain is "pointer-chasing" through the UpValue hash table plus tight floating-/fixed-point dependency chains. This is latency-bound work with serial data dependencies — it does not shard across chips or even across cores. PICO-8's virtual CPU budget is **8,388,608 cycles/sec (2^23), ≈139,810 cycles/frame at 60fps** (confirmed by the PICO-8 Wiki), at ~2 cycles per Lua VM instruction → a target near **4.2 million Lua VM instructions/second** (PICO-8 "runs at roughly 4MHz… 4 × 1024 × 1024 = 4,194,304" per Lexaloffle BBS). The fix for missing that target is a faster interpreter core, full stop.

**2. The ESP32-P4 is materially faster on exactly the axes that matter.**
- **CPU (CoreMark, official Espressif datasheets):** P4 dual-core **2489.62 CoreMark @ 360 MHz (6.92 CoreMark/MHz)** vs. S3 dual-core **1181.60 CoreMark @ 240 MHz (4.92 CoreMark/MHz)**; S3 single-core is **613.86 CoreMark @ 240 MHz (2.56 CoreMark/MHz)** per ESP32-S3 datasheet v1.9. Per-core, the P4's RISC-V HP core is ~1.35× the S3's Xtensa LX7 per-MHz and ~2.0× per core at stock clocks (360 vs 240 MHz); ~2.25× at the promised 400 MHz. *(Caveat: Espressif's newer S3 datasheet v2.2 revises the S3 dual-core figure up to 1329.92 / 5.54 CoreMark/MHz, likely a newer toolchain — a ~13% change that does not alter the conclusion.)*
- **Memory:** S3 octal PSRAM measures **~84 MB/s** when exceeding cache (vs 160 MB/s theoretical); P4 measures **~185 MB/s PSRAM→SRAM at 200 MHz PSRAM** (near its ~200 MB/s ceiling) and ~775 MB/s SRAM→SRAM. The P4 also has **768 KB on-chip SRAM/L2** vs the S3's 512 KB, so more of the hot Lua heap stays cached. For pointer-chasing this cache + bandwidth advantage is as important as clock.
- **Realistic Lua speedup estimate:** combining ~1.35× IPC-per-MHz, 1.5–1.67× clock, and ~2.2× memory bandwidth/latency headroom, expect **~1.7–2.2× end-to-end** for the branch-heavy, cache-unfriendly Lua workload — enough to move a borderline-30fps heavy cart into 60fps territory. This is *derived*, not measured; real-world corroboration: RetroGo SNES emulation reaches ~55/60fps on the P4 (360 MHz) where the S3 is flagged "slow," and an ESP32-forum compute-loop test measured "~2.5× faster than S3."

**3. LuaJIT is not an option on either chip, so this stays an interpreter problem.** There is no upstream LuaJIT Xtensa backend, and the RISC-V port (PR #1267 by infiWang/PLCT Lab) targets **RV64 (LP64D) Linux**, not the RV32 bare-metal MCU profile of the P4. So even on the P4 you are running the plain z8lua interpreter — which is precisely why raw interpreter throughput (CoreMark-like) is the governing metric.

**4. The 2 MiB Lua-heap requirement (stat(0)) forces PSRAM and kills small-SRAM MCUs.** This is what defeated both RP2040 PICO-8 ports; yocto-8's own docs now state verbatim: "RP2350 with PSRAM is recommended. Because PICO-8 emulates a Lua heap limit of 2MB; no amount of magic will allow all games run on a MCU with a tenth of that." Both the S3 (8 MB PSRAM) and P4 (up to 32 MB in-package) satisfy this; the question is purely *how fast* they walk that heap.

**5. The co-processor compute-offload idea is unsound — here is the arithmetic.**
- **You cannot offload the Lua VM piecemeal.** The heap is mutated with tight serial dependencies. Any scheme that ships heap state over an interconnect pays a round-trip per dependency. Even a fast 80 MHz quad-SPI link (~40 MB/s realistic) moving the full 2 MB heap once would cost ~50 ms — 3× the entire frame budget. The workload is anti-parallel.
- **You cannot cleanly split _update() from _draw() either.** `_draw` calls (spr, map, line, rectfill, tline, fillp) are *issued by* the Lua code, interleaved with state reads. A "display-list/command-FIFO" split would stream draw commands over the link — feasible in bandwidth (a heavy cart issues low-tens-of-KB of draw commands/frame) but it does *not* remove the Lua bottleneck, which is the actual problem; it only relocates the already-cheap rasterization. Historical precedents (SNES SuperFX, Sega 32X, Amiga blitter) worked because the *host* code was the fast part and the co-processor did bounded, embarrassingly-parallel pixel/vector work — the inverse of PICO-8, where the slow part (Lua) is the part you cannot move.
- **The framebuffer handoff is cheap but pointless.** 128×128 @ 4bpp = 8 KB/frame → 480 KB/s at 60fps: trivially within any SPI link. But the S3's Core 1 *already* does palette expansion + integer scaling + panel feeding cheaply and is not saturated (PicoPico moved exactly this to the second core and it "no longer blocked the render cycle"). Offloading already-idle-core work to a second chip buys a sync problem and BOM cost and zero throughput.
- **Sync/coherency tax is real:** FreeRTOS task handoff, SPI-DMA setup, and PSRAM cache flushes for coherency each cost microseconds-to-tens-of-microseconds per frame; a two-chip split adds overhead to a budget you are trying to protect.

**6. The one legitimate co-processor is a radio companion — and the P4 needs it regardless.** Since the P4 has no radio, the standard, Espressif-blessed solution is an **ESP32-C6** (or C5) over **SDIO** running **esp_hosted / ESP-Hosted-MCU** firmware with the **esp_wifi_remote** host API. Measured on a Waveshare ESP32-P4-WIFI6-NANO board: **~36 Mbps real-world WiFi throughput** over a 40 MHz SDIO link (the SDIO transport itself does ~79.5 Mbps, so the WiFi PHY — not the link — is the ceiling), with esp-hosted adding "only ~3% wire overhead" for normal STA traffic. This is more than enough for BBS cart downloads and OTA. This is the "co-processor" that makes sense — a fixed-function radio, not a compute helper — and it collapses your two options into one: choosing the P4 *is* choosing a (radio) co-processor architecture.

**7. Important negative result on the P4's PPA (Pixel-Processing Accelerator).** The PPA **cannot** do PICO-8's core pixel operation — 4-bit-indexed → RGB565 palette expansion — in hardware. Per the ESP-IDF PPA reference, its SRM (scale/rotate/mirror), blend, and fill engines accept only RGB565/RGB888/ARGB8888, YUV, GRAY8, and alpha-only masks — the A4/A8 modes are a 4-bit *alpha* channel paired with a single fixed RGB color, **not** a 16-entry CLUT/palette. So the PPA does *not* offload the indexed-color blit; that stays on the CPU (which does it cheaply anyway). The PPA *can* do the final **integer 2×/3× upscale** of an already-expanded RGB565 128×128 buffer to the panel, and hardware fill/blend — but Espressif and LVGL both note PPA gains are **memory-bandwidth-bound** ("PPA performance highly relies on the PSRAM bandwidth… performance will be greatly reduced" when other peripherals contend), and LVGL reports blend acceleration currently yields "no significant gains" for that reason. Net: the P4's value here is CPU + bandwidth + MIPI-DSI display path, **not** the PPA magically rasterizing PICO-8.

**8. Can the S3 be made to hit 60fps anyway? Partially — with real work, and not for the hardest carts.** Optimization ceiling on the S3:
- Keep Lua VM hot state and most-accessed heap objects in **internal SRAM** (yocto-8's author explicitly found "the less SRAM yocto-8 would use, the faster it could run, because the more heap data could be stuffed into SRAM"); a custom arena allocator to improve locality; the **120 MHz octal PSRAM** option (note: 120 MHz needs temperature-compensated timing calibration and is flagged experimental); 64-byte cache-line tuning; -O2/-O3; computed-goto / jump-table dispatch in the interpreter core (**measured 2026-07-14 on the S3: `-fjump-tables` gives ~2.8–3.1× — ESP-IDF's `-fno-jump-tables` default compiles z8lua's opcode switch as a linear compare-chain and was handicapping the interpreter that much; a one-line build flag, no source change. See** [`worklog/2026-07-14-phase0-gate2-luabench.md`](../worklog/2026-07-14-phase0-gate2-luabench.md)); and precompiled bytecode (PicoPico's to_c.py already does this: parse time 112 ms → 18 ms for a large cart, and bytecode stays in flash — note: a **load-time** win, it does not raise steady-state throughput).
- **Lua→C AOT transpilation** (David Ventura's "lua-but-worse") is the highest-upside path but is **incomplete and buggy** by the author's own account ("It is promising, but buggy") — it is a research project, not a shippable dependency.
- **Frame breakdown data is thin.** The clearest published numbers: PicoPico "hello world" _draw ~9 ms and framebuffer copy ~12 ms → ~5 ms after SPI clock bump on an RP2040; Celeste 16.21 ms → 13.94 ms/frame after palette pre-encoding on an overclocked (260 MHz) RP2040. There is no clean published Lua-vs-draw-vs-audio split for a heavy _update60 cart on an S3. The zepto8 synth converted to fix32 dropped an example SFX from ~25 ms → ~2 ms, so audio is *not* the bottleneck once fixed-point.

**9. How many carts actually need 60fps?** PICO-8 defaults to 30fps (`_update`/`_draw`); a cart opts into 60fps only by defining `_update60()`. The 60fps carts are a **minority** of the library, but they include some marquee/action titles, and your stated target is explicitly full 60fps compatibility. Dropping heavy `_update60` carts to 30fps is the graceful degradation PICO-8 itself performs (stat(1) > 1 → frames dropped), so a 30fps fallback is not catastrophic — but it fails your stated bar.

### Why "faster core, not more cores" is the correct framing
PICO-8's model is a single virtual CPU. The runtime is: read inputs → run `_update`/`_update60` (Lua, serial, heap-mutating) → run `_draw` (Lua issuing C++ API calls) → rasterize → flip → mix 4 audio channels. Of these, only rasterization and audio are parallelizable, and both are already cheap and already fit on the S3's second core. The expensive, irreducible part is the Lua interpreter loop, which has a critical path you cannot cut with more silicon — only with a faster path through one core's pipeline and memory system. That is the textbook definition of a workload where you buy a faster core.

### The RP2350-as-Lua-host trap
It is tempting because the Pimoroni Pico Plus 2 has 8 MB PSRAM (solving the heap-size problem). But: (a) a 150 MHz M33 (~4.09 CoreMark/MHz → ~614 CoreMark) is roughly equal to a *single* S3 core and well below a P4 core; (b) RP2350 external PSRAM is QSPI with poor random-access characteristics — measured **~16–28 MB/s cached, ~27–46 MB/s uncached**, and "write performance when mapping to the cached region is horrible." For a pointer-chasing Lua heap that random-access latency is disqualifying. The RP2350 is a fine *display/IO* chip; it is a poor *Lua host*, and a poor *co-processor* for the reasons in Finding 5.

### WiFi on the P4: real but with rough edges
The esp_hosted/esp_wifi_remote stack works and is officially supported, but 2026 field reports show integration friction: association instability on some ESPHome P4+C6 setups, and a known failure mode where "WiFi and display used simultaneously" can reset the board, plus MIPI-DSI "can't fetch data from external memory fast enough" underruns when PSRAM is contended. These are solvable (buffer placement, `use_psram` for the SDIO pool, PSRAM at 200 MHz) but budget integration time. Boards that pre-integrate the pair: **Waveshare ESP32-P4-Nano / -WIFI6 / Module-DEV-KIT**, **M5Stack Tab5** (P4 + C6-MINI, 1280×720 DSI, ~$55) and **M5Stamp-P4** ($12.95 module + $7 C6 add-on). Bare P4 is ~$3–4 in qty.

### Cost / effort deltas
- **P4 + C6:** higher BOM (~two chips + SDIO routing), moderate new-platform porting effort (write the fake-08 host layer once; ESP-IDF supports P4 fully). Biggest risk: driver maturity for MIPI-DSI + WiFi coexistence.
- **S3 alone (optimized):** lowest BOM, but you are betting the project on squeezing ~1.5–2× out of interpreter/allocator optimization that may not fully land for the hardest `_update60` carts. Realistic outcome: most 30fps carts and many 60fps carts run great; a tail of heavy 60fps carts drop to 30fps.
- **Pi Zero 2 W:** lowest software risk (official binary, WiFi, Splore for BBS), but "it's a computer" — multi-second boot, higher idle power, larger battery, and it forfeits the MCU-build satisfaction. Instant-on and long battery life are the things you give up.

## Recommendations

**Primary recommendation: build on the ESP32-P4 + ESP32-C6, and drop the compute-co-processor idea entirely.** It is the only MCU path with a credible shot at full 60fps compatibility while retaining WiFi/BLE, and it keeps your architecture to "one fast compute core + one radio companion" — the co-processor topology that is actually justified.

**Decision tree (run the gates in order):**

- **Gate 1 — Define the bar honestly.** If "full-cart 60fps with zero compatibility caveats" is a hard product requirement (not a craft goal), **stop and use a Raspberry Pi Zero 2 W with the official PICO-8 binary.** It meets the bar today. Proceed to the MCU gates only if instant-on, battery life, and the MCU-class build are themselves goals worth engineering risk for.

- **Gate 2 — Benchmark z8lua single-thread throughput on real silicon before committing.** Buy an **M5Stack Tab5 (~$55, P4+C6+DSI screen)** or a **Waveshare ESP32-P4-Nano**, and an S3 devboard you likely already have. Port the z8lua interpreter core (no display, no audio) and run a fixed workload: the `_update60` of 3–4 known-heavy carts for N frames, measuring pure Lua ms/frame with the heap in PSRAM.
  - **If P4 Lua time ≤ ~14 ms/frame** on your heavy carts (leaving margin for draw+audio+flip within 16.6 ms) → **commit to the P4.** Expected, given ~2× headroom over the S3 and Celeste already fitting on S3-class hardware.
  - **If P4 is 14–20 ms/frame** → commit to P4 but plan the SRAM-arena allocator (hot heap objects in the 768 KB on-chip SRAM) and accept a small tail of carts at 30fps.
  - **If even the P4 is > 20 ms/frame on your target carts** → no MCU will do it; fall back to the Pi Zero 2 W.

- **Gate 3 — Do NOT prototype any compute co-processor.** The arithmetic (Finding 5) rules it out a priori. The only "co-processor" you build is the C6 radio, which the P4 requires anyway.

- **Gate 4 — WiFi integration validation.** On the chosen P4 board, validate esp_wifi_remote + display coexistence early (place SDIO buffers in PSRAM via `use_psram`, run PSRAM at 200 MHz, confirm no DSI underrun/reset under simultaneous WiFi + full-frame rendering). This is the P4 path's top integration risk.

**Benchmarks/thresholds that would change the recommendation:**
- If a maintained **RV32 bare-metal LuaJIT** backend appears for the P4, revisit — JIT would blow the throughput question wide open (currently not available).
- If S3 Gate-2 numbers come back ≤ ~13 ms/frame on your heaviest carts (possible with bytecode precompile + SRAM arena + 120 MHz PSRAM), the cheaper single-chip S3 becomes viable and you can skip the C6/SDIO complexity — but treat 120 MHz PSRAM's thermal-calibration caveat as a reliability risk.
- If your final cart set turns out to contain few/no heavy `_update60` titles, the whole 60fps-headroom argument weakens and the S3 suffices.

## Caveats
- **Measured vs. derived vs. speculative:** CoreMark scores, PSRAM bandwidths (S3 ~84 MB/s, P4 ~185 MB/s, RP2350 ~16–46 MB/s), WiFi throughput (~36 Mbps), and PicoPico frame numbers are **measured/published**. The "~1.7–2.2× Lua speedup" for the P4 is **derived** from CoreMark + memory characteristics, not a measured Lua benchmark on both chips — Gate 2 exists specifically to replace this estimate with data. Statements about specific carts hitting 60fps on the P4 are **projections** until benchmarked.
- **Datasheet conflict:** the S3 dual-core CoreMark is 1181.60 (older datasheets, v1.x) vs 1329.92 (v2.2) — a ~13% revision, likely toolchain. It does not change the qualitative conclusion.
- **P4 clock:** most shipping P4 boards run at **360 MHz**; the 400 MHz figure is Espressif's spec ceiling and not yet universally delivered. Benchmark at the clock your board actually runs.
- **PPA limitation is firm:** it does not do indexed→RGB565 palette expansion; do not architect around the PPA rasterizing PICO-8. Its useful role is the final integer upscale + panel path, and even that is PSRAM-bandwidth-bound.
- **No shipping PICO-8 port on the P4 yet:** you are the early adopter for fake-08-on-P4. RetroGo/DOOM prior art proves the chip is capable and ESP-IDF-mature, but you will be writing the host/platform layer and debugging DSI+WiFi coexistence yourself.
- **AOT transpilation is not a dependency you can rely on today** — David Ventura's lua-but-worse is explicitly incomplete.
